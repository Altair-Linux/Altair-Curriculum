## Introduction

Every time you interact with a computer, something physical happens first. Your finger moves. Your voice makes sound. Your eyes look at a screen and your hand follows. An input device is the part of the computer that detects that physical action and converts it into information the computer can process. Without input devices, a computer would just sit there running its programs with no way for a person to communicate with it. Today we are going to look closely at how different input devices work and practise using two of them accurately.

## What We Will Learn

- What an input device is and what job it does.
- How different physical actions are converted into computer instructions.
- How a mouse, touchscreen, keyboard, and microphone each work.
- How to control a mouse or touchscreen to do a specific task.

## Core Explanation

An input device detects something from the physical world and converts it into data. That data goes to the processor, which decides what to do with it.

A **keyboard** detects which key is pressed. Each key sends a unique code. The computer matches the code to a character and displays it on screen, or uses it as a command.

A **mouse** detects movement and button clicks. As you move the mouse, it tracks how far it has moved in two directions: left-right and up-down. It sends that movement data to the computer, which moves the cursor by the same amount. Clicking sends a separate signal: left click, right click, or scroll wheel.

A **touchscreen** works differently. It detects where on the screen your finger is touching by sensing the electrical charge in your skin. It knows the position of your touch, how many fingers are touching, and how much pressure is applied. A tap is like a click. A swipe moves through content. A pinch zooms in or out.

A **microphone** converts sound waves into electrical signals. The computer analyses those signals to detect words, volume, or other audio information.

A **camera** detects light. It converts the pattern of light coming through the lens into pixel data that the computer can store or display.

Each of these devices turns a physical action into a piece of data. That data then triggers a sequence of actions in the program.

## Activity

**Input Device Exploration and Precision Challenge** (30 minutes)

Part 1: Input device identification (10 minutes)

Step 1 (5 minutes): Show the class a keyboard, mouse, and tablet (or pictures of each). For each device, ask: "What physical action does this detect? What does that action do on the screen?" Build a three-column table on the board: Device, Physical action, On-screen result.

Keyboard: pressing a key, a letter or command appears.
Mouse: moving the mouse, cursor moves. Clicking the button, an item is selected or opened.
Touchscreen: tapping with finger, item is selected or opened. Swiping, page scrolls.
Microphone: speaking a word, voice assistant responds or text appears.

Step 2 (5 minutes): Ask: "Which of these inputs is most like giving a spoken instruction? (Microphone.) Which is most like pointing? (Mouse or touch.) Which is most like writing? (Keyboard.)" Connect back to input and output from the earlier lesson.

Part 2: Precision mouse or touchscreen challenge (20 minutes)

Step 3 (5 minutes): Open a simple drawing or puzzle app on each device. Demonstrate: "Move the cursor slowly to the edge of the screen without clicking anything. Now click exactly on this small target." Show that precision matters. Clicking to the left of a button does not activate it.

Step 4 (10 minutes): Pairs take turns with two challenges.

Challenge 1 (mouse): Draw a straight horizontal line across the screen using the drawing tool. Then draw a square. Discuss what makes this difficult. (Steady hand, precise start and end points.)

Challenge 2 (touchscreen or mouse): In a puzzle app or drag-and-drop activity, drag an item from one side of the screen to a specific target on the other side. Release it exactly on the target.

Step 5 (5 minutes): Class discussion. Ask: "Which felt easier to control? What made precise control difficult?" Connect to programming: "When a programmer designs a button, they think about how big it needs to be to make it easy to click. This is a design decision based on how input devices work."

## Real-World Connection

A surgeon sometimes uses a robot arm controlled by their hand movements. Their hands are the input device. The robot detects each movement and replicates it on a smaller scale. Game controllers use similar input technology: each button, stick, and trigger sends a signal to the game, which translates it into an action for the player's character.

## Check for Understanding

1. A mouse detects two kinds of physical action. What are they?
2. What does a touchscreen detect that a regular screen does not?
3. You design a button for a tablet app. The button is only 2 pixels wide. Why would this be a problem for users?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Focus on the touchscreen only. Use a large-target drag-and-drop activity. Name the physical action (tap, drag, release) for each step before doing it.

**For students who want a challenge:** Research one unusual input device, such as an eye-tracking device used by people who cannot use their hands, or a motion sensor used in a games console. Describe what physical action it detects and how that translates to computer data.

## Summary

- An input device detects a physical action and converts it into data the computer can use.
- Keyboards detect key presses. Mice detect movement and clicks. Touchscreens detect finger position. Microphones detect sound.
- Precise use of input devices takes practice. The accuracy of your input directly affects what the computer does.
- Programmers consider how input devices work when designing apps and buttons.
