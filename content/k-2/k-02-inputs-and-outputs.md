## Introduction

Last lesson we learned that a computer takes in information and gives back a result. Today we are going to look more carefully at the parts that do those two jobs. When something goes into a computer, we call it an input. When the computer gives something back to us, we call it an output. These two words are used by everyone who works with computers, from Kindergarten all the way to professional engineers.

## What We Will Learn

- What the word input means and how to spot an input.
- What the word output means and how to spot an output.
- Examples of inputs and outputs from devices we already know.
- How input and output work together like a conversation.

## Core Explanation

Think about talking to a friend. You say something (that is your input to your friend). Your friend listens, thinks, and then replies (that is their output back to you). A computer works the same way.

An **input** is any way of giving information to a computer. When you type on a keyboard, the keys are input. When you tap a touchscreen, your finger is giving input. When you speak to a voice assistant, your voice is input. When you take a photo with a camera, the image going in is input.

An **output** is any way the computer gives information back to you. Words and pictures on a screen are output. Sound from a speaker is output. A printed page from a printer is output. A phone vibrating in your hand is output.

Some parts do both jobs. A touchscreen on a tablet is an input because your finger presses on it. But the touchscreen is also part of the display, so it shows output too. We call this an input-output part, but for now the most important thing is recognising the two roles separately.

A simple way to remember the difference: input goes in, output comes out. The arrow points into the computer for input, and out of the computer for output.

## Activity

**The Input-Output Sorting Game** (25 minutes)

Materials: two labelled boxes or sheets of paper, picture cards for keyboard, mouse, microphone, touchscreen, speaker, screen, printer, and camera.

Step 1 (3 minutes): Place the two boxes in the front of the room. Read the labels aloud: INPUT and OUTPUT. Ask students to repeat each word. Ask: "Has anyone heard these words before? What do you think they mean?"

Step 2 (5 minutes): Pick up the keyboard card. Say: "When I type, where does the information go? Into the computer. So a keyboard is an INPUT." Place it in the box. Pick up the speaker card. Say: "When the computer plays a sound, does that information go in or come out? It comes out to us. So a speaker is an OUTPUT." Place it in the box.

Step 3 (12 minutes): Give each pair of students one card. Students discuss together, then come to the front and place their card in the correct box. They must say one reason. "The microphone is an input because it sends your voice into the computer." If they are unsure, encourage the class to help.

Step 4 (5 minutes): Once all cards are placed, review together. Ask: "Is there anything we want to move?" Discuss the camera and the touchscreen as interesting cases where students may disagree. Both are valid discussions.

**Body Input-Output (bonus, 5 minutes):** Ask students to stand up. Say: "Your eyes are your inputs. They take in information. Your voice is an output. It sends information out." Call out different body parts (ears, hands, nose, mouth, eyes) and have students shout "INPUT" or "OUTPUT."

## Real-World Connection

When you press the button on a TV remote, your finger is the input. The remote sends a signal to the TV, and the TV changes the channel. What you see on the screen is the output. You do this without thinking about it, but you are using input and output every time.

## Check for Understanding

1. You are watching a video on a tablet and you turn up the volume by pressing a button. Which part is the input and which part is the output?
2. Can you think of a time today when you gave input to a machine?
3. If a computer had no output at all, what would be the problem with using it?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use just two examples: a keyboard as input and a screen as output. Have the student physically mime typing (input) and then look at an imaginary screen (output). Repeat with a microphone and a speaker.

**For students who want a challenge:** Think of a device that has more than one input and more than one output. A games console, for example, takes input from the controller and also from the internet. It outputs to the screen and to the speakers. Draw a diagram showing all the inputs and outputs for that device.

## Summary

- An input is how information goes into a computer. Keyboards, microphones, cameras, and touchscreens are all inputs.
- An output is how the computer sends information back to us. Screens, speakers, and printers are all outputs.
- Input and output work together. Without input, the computer has nothing to work with. Without output, we cannot see or hear what the computer has done.
