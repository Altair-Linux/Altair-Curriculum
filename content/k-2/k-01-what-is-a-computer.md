## Introduction

You have probably pressed a button on a screen, typed on a keyboard, or watched a video on a tablet. All of those things involve a computer. A computer is a machine that takes in information, does something with it, and gives back a result. Computers come in many shapes. Some sit on desks. Some fit in a pocket. Some are hidden inside a car or a washing machine. Today we are going to find out what makes something a computer and what computers actually do.

## What We Will Learn

- What a computer is and what it does.
- That computers need instructions to work.
- The difference between a part that goes in (input) and a part that comes out (output).
- That computers are found in many everyday objects.

## Core Explanation

A computer is a machine that follows instructions. It cannot think on its own. When you tap a screen, you are giving the computer an instruction. The computer reads that instruction and does exactly what it says.

Every computer has three main jobs. It takes in information, it processes that information (works out what to do), and it gives back a result.

The parts that bring information in are called **input** parts. A keyboard is an input. A touchscreen is an input. A microphone is an input.

The parts that show or play the result are called **output** parts. A screen is an output. A speaker is an output. A printer is an output.

There is also a part that does the thinking. It is called the processor, or the brain of the computer. It reads the instructions and makes everything happen.

Here is a simple way to think about it. You clap your hands near a smart speaker (input). The speaker hears you (processor reads the instruction). Music starts playing (output). The computer did its job.

Not every machine is a computer. A flashlight has a switch and a bulb, but nothing inside reads instructions or processes information. A calculator that only adds numbers is much simpler than a computer, which can run many different programs.

## Activity

**The Computer or Not Game** (20 minutes)

Materials: printed or drawn pictures of a tablet, a phone, a television, a car, a flashlight, a microwave, a basic calculator, and a digital alarm clock. One per pair or small group, face down in a pile.

Step 1 (2 minutes): Set up two areas on the floor or table. Label one area "Computer" and one area "Not a Computer". Use cards or paper signs.

Step 2 (3 minutes): Show students a real device, such as a tablet or laptop. Point to the screen and say "this shows us the result, so it is an output." Point to the keyboard or touchscreen and say "this is where information goes in, so it is an input." Let students repeat the words input and output.

Step 3 (10 minutes): In pairs, students take turns picking a picture card from the pile. They decide together whether the device is a computer or not. They place it in the correct area and say one reason for their choice. For example, "a phone is a computer because you can put in different instructions and it does different things."

Step 4 (5 minutes): Bring the class together. Review any cards that caused disagreement. The microwave and digital alarm clock are interesting cases because they do contain small processors, but they only run one program. Use this to discuss: a full computer can run many different programs.

Correct answers to aim for: tablet, phone, laptop, car, smart television = computers. Flashlight, basic flashcard calculator, hammer = not computers.

## Real-World Connection

The computer in a car reads information from sensors on the wheels and engine, processes it, and shows warning lights on the dashboard. Without that computer, the car could not warn the driver about problems. Computers are doing quiet, important jobs all around us, even when we cannot see a screen.

## Check for Understanding

1. A student says a flashlight is a computer because it uses electricity. How would you explain that a flashlight is not a computer?
2. Point to something in the room. Is any part of it an input or an output?
3. If you tap a video on a tablet and the video plays, which part was the input and which part was the output?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Focus only on the words input and output. Use your body as an example. Your ears take in sound (input). Your mouth speaks words (output). Draw a simple arrow diagram together showing information going in and coming out.

**For students who want a challenge:** Find three things at home that you think might have a small computer inside. For each one, describe what the input is and what the output is. Share your findings with the class.

## Summary

- A computer is a machine that follows instructions to do a job.
- Computers have input parts that take in information and output parts that give back results.
- Computers are found in phones, cars, tablets, televisions, and many other devices.
