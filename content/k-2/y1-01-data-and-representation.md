## Introduction

Every time you use a computer, it is working with data. Data is just information stored in a way the computer can read and use. A list of names is data. A number showing how many apples are in a box is data. A photo is data. Even the tally your teacher makes when she counts how many students brought lunch is a simple form of data. Today we are going to collect some data as a class, organise it, and find out what it can tell us.

## What We Will Learn

- What data is and where it comes from.
- How to collect data by asking questions and recording answers.
- How to show data in a chart so it is easy to read.
- How to use a chart to answer simple questions.

## Core Explanation

**What is data?**

Data is information. It can be a word, a number, a picture, or a sound. When something is stored so it can be used again, counted, or compared, it is data.

Not all information is data yet. If you look out the window and notice it is raining, that is information in your head. When you write "raining: yes" in a notebook, you have turned that observation into data.

Computers store data in files. A text file stores words. An image file stores the colour of every tiny dot in a picture. A music file stores the sound waves of every note.

**Collecting and organising data**

Raw data is just a pile of facts. To make sense of it, you organise it. One common way is a **tally**: you make a mark for each item and count the marks. Another way is a **pictograph**: you use one picture or symbol to represent each item. A **table** puts the information into rows and columns.

Organised data is easier to read and easier to ask questions about. "We collected 25 answers. 14 people said cats, 9 people said dogs, 2 people said fish." Those three facts came from organising 25 separate pieces of raw data.

## Activity

**Class Favourites Chart** (28 minutes)

Step 1 (5 minutes): Ask the class a simple question: "What is your favourite animal: cat, dog, or bird?" Each student takes a sticky note and draws or writes their answer. No one discusses their answer yet.

Step 2 (8 minutes): Collect the sticky notes. On the whiteboard, draw three columns labelled Cat, Dog, and Bird. Stick each note in the correct column. Ask students to help you count each column. Write the total at the bottom.

Step 3 (5 minutes): Ask questions based on the chart. "Which animal got the most votes? How do you know from looking at the chart?" "How many more people chose cats than dogs?" "If we asked three more students and they all said bird, what would change?"

Step 4 (5 minutes): Each student now creates their own tally on their sheet using the class data. They make a tally mark for each vote and then count the total. Check that everyone ends up with the same totals.

Step 5 (5 minutes): Show how the same data could be shown as a pictograph. Draw one small paw print for each cat vote, one dog face for each dog vote, and so on. Ask: "Which is easier to read at a glance, the tally or the pictograph? Which is easier to count exactly?"

## Real-World Connection

Weather stations collect temperature data every hour, every day, all year. This data is stored in computers that can show you graphs of temperature over time. Without organised data, a weather forecast would be a guess. With data, it is a calculated prediction based on thousands of measurements.

## Check for Understanding

1. You ask ten friends what their favourite colour is. Six say blue, three say red, one says yellow. Show this data as a tally.
2. Look at a chart showing: apples 8, bananas 3, grapes 6. Which fruit is the least popular? How much more popular are apples than bananas?
3. Why is it more useful to store information as data than to just try to remember it?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use a two-choice question only (for example, cats or dogs). Work side by side to make the tally. Count out loud together. Focus on reading one fact from the completed chart.

**For students who want a challenge:** Collect your own data question from a group of eight students. Record the answers as a tally, then draw a pictograph, then write three facts your chart shows. Can you think of a question that the chart cannot answer even though the data is there?

## Summary

- Data is information stored in a way that can be counted, organised, and compared.
- You collect data by observing or asking questions and recording the answers.
- Organising data into a tally, pictograph, or table makes it easier to read and understand.
- Charts let you answer questions about data quickly.
