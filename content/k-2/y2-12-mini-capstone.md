## Introduction

This is the capstone lesson for the K to 2 curriculum. You have learned what a computer is, how inputs and outputs work, how to write a sequence, how to use loops and conditionals, how to debug, how to collect and display data, and how to work with a partner on a shared project. Today you are going to bring all of those ideas together in one original project. You will design something, plan it carefully, and present it. The goal is not to build the most complex thing possible. The goal is to show that you understand the ideas clearly enough to explain them to someone else.

## What We Will Learn

- How to define a specific problem worth solving with a computational approach.
- How to produce a complete project plan using everything learned this year and in previous years.
- How to present a technical plan to an audience in clear, confident language.
- How to give structured feedback on a peer's plan.

## Core Explanation

**Defining the problem**

A good project starts with a clear problem statement. Not "I want to make a game" but "I want to make a program that tests whether you know your two-times table, keeps your score, and tells you how many you got right at the end." The more specific the problem, the easier it is to plan a solution.

**The project plan**

A complete plan has five parts.

The **title** names the project.

The **problem statement** says what the project does and who it is for in two or three sentences.

The **algorithm** describes the steps the project follows, in order, using sequence notation, loop notation, and at least one conditional. This is the core of the plan.

The **inputs and outputs** list what the user provides and what the program produces in return.

The **diagram** is a flowchart or annotated sketch showing the structure of the program visually.

**Presenting the plan**

Presenting means explaining your thinking to someone who has not seen your plan before. You walk through each part in order. You do not read from your sheet, you explain it in your own words. You point to the part of your diagram you are talking about. You are ready for one or two questions.

**Structured peer feedback**

Feedback must be specific and constructive. "It is good" is not feedback. "Your algorithm includes a loop but I could not find a conditional. You could add one here" is feedback. The checklist guides this.

Peer feedback checklist:
1. Is the problem specific enough to know when the project is finished?
2. Does the algorithm include a sequence, a loop, and a conditional?
3. Are both inputs and outputs listed?
4. Is there a diagram or flowchart?
5. One thing that is clear and well-explained.
6. One question I still have about the project.

## Activity

**Plan, Build, Present** (50 minutes)

Part 1: Project selection and planning (20 minutes)

Step 1 (5 minutes): Present the project options list. Students may choose one of these or propose their own (subject to teacher approval).

Option A: A quiz program. The program asks three questions, stores the score, and at the end prints the score and a message that depends on how many were correct.

Option B: A daily routine helper. The program guides a character through a morning routine, using a loop for any repeated task and a conditional for a step that depends on a variable (for example, if the weather variable is "rainy," then add "bring umbrella").

Option C: A simple data display. The program asks the user to enter three numbers, stores them in variables, finds the largest, and displays a simple text chart.

Option D: A two-scene interactive story with at least two decision points.

Step 2 (15 minutes): Each student completes their planning sheet. Teacher circulates and checks each plan before students proceed to building. A plan may not move to the building phase until it includes: a problem statement, an algorithm with all three structures, inputs and outputs, and a diagram.

Part 2: Build or extend (15 minutes)

Step 3 (15 minutes): Students who have devices build their project in ScratchJr or Scratch, following their plan exactly. Students without devices write the program in pseudocode on a second sheet of paper, line by line, following their plan. Either form counts as a completed project.

Part 3: Present and give feedback (15 minutes)

Step 4 (15 minutes): Groups of three take turns presenting. Each student has two to three minutes. While one student presents, the other two complete the peer feedback checklist. After all three have presented, each student receives both checklists from their audience. Allow two minutes for the presenter to read the feedback silently.

Teacher selects one or two plans to highlight for the whole class. Point out what makes the plan strong: the specificity of the problem, the correct use of all three structures, the clarity of the diagram.

## Real-World Connection

Every piece of software that exists started as a plan. Professional software teams write technical specifications before they write a single line of code. The specification describes the problem, the algorithm, the inputs and outputs, and the structure of the program. The better the specification, the smoother the build. A weak plan produces a confused program. A clear plan produces something that works.

## Check for Understanding

1. Your project plan says "the program asks the user a question." Is this input or output? What about "the program shows the result on screen?"
2. A peer's plan includes a sequence and a loop but no conditional. What would you write on their feedback checklist, and where in their algorithm might they add one?
3. Why is it important to define the problem before planning the algorithm?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Provide a project plan template with sentence starters in every section. Reduce the algorithm requirement to sequence and one conditional only. Focus on completing a clear, honest plan rather than an ambitious one.

**For students who want a challenge:** After the presentation, build the project fully and test it with a user who was not in the planning session. Record one bug you found during testing, the strategy you used to find it, and the fix. Write a V2 note explaining what changed between the plan and the final build.

## Summary

- A capstone project brings together sequence, loops, conditionals, variables, inputs, outputs, and debugging in one original piece of work.
- A complete plan has a problem statement, an algorithm, a list of inputs and outputs, and a diagram or flowchart.
- Presenting a plan clearly means explaining each part in your own words to someone who has not seen it before.
- Structured feedback is specific and actionable. It identifies what is strong and asks one precise question about what is unclear.
