## Introduction

In Kindergarten, you learned how to look after a device and keep your personal information safe. Year 1 takes this further. When you use technology to talk to someone, send a message, or post something, you are making choices about how you treat other people. Those choices matter just as much online as they do in person. The word for being a responsible, respectful, and safe technology user is **digital citizen**. Today we are going to look at what good digital citizenship looks like in real situations.

## What We Will Learn

- What a digital citizen is and why it matters how we behave online.
- That words and images sent online can affect real people and should be chosen carefully.
- What personal information is and why we protect it.
- How to recognise responsible and irresponsible technology use.

## Core Explanation

**Online communication is still communication between real people.**

When you type a message or comment, a real person reads it. If the message is unkind, that person feels hurt, just as they would if you said it to their face. The fact that you are not in the same room does not change the effect of the words.

Being a good digital citizen means treating others online the way you would want to be treated in person. It means thinking before you send, share, or say something. If you would not say it to someone standing next to you, you should not type it to them either.

**Privacy is still important.**

You learned in Kindergarten that your full name, address, and school name are private. This is still true, and the list is a little longer now. Your photos, your location, your passwords, and your family members' names are also private. You should not share these with anyone you do not already know in real life, no matter how friendly they seem online.

**Responsible use of devices and content.**

Using a device responsibly means using it for the purpose it was given to you for, at the times you are allowed, and not using it to do something that would harm or upset someone else. It also means being careful about what you photograph and share. Taking a photo of someone without their permission and sharing it is not respectful, even if the photo seems harmless.

## Activity

**Digital Citizens: Responsible or Irresponsible?** (28 minutes)

Part 1: T-chart sort (15 minutes)

Give each pair a T-chart labelled Responsible and Irresponsible. Read out eight scenarios one at a time. After each one, pairs decide which column it belongs in and write the scenario number. They must be ready to explain their choice.

Scenario 1: You receive a mean comment on a drawing you shared. You tell a trusted adult. (Responsible.)
Scenario 2: You find your classmate's photo on a tablet and send it to everyone in the class without asking. (Irresponsible: no permission, could embarrass the classmate.)
Scenario 3: You use the school tablet to practise your typing during free time. (Responsible.)
Scenario 4: You type "you're stupid" in a message to a classmate because you are upset. (Irresponsible: unkind, hurtful even online.)
Scenario 5: A stranger online asks for your home address to send you a prize. You close the message and tell an adult. (Responsible.)
Scenario 6: You take a photo of a friend making a silly face and share it on a class app without asking them. (Irresponsible: no consent.)
Scenario 7: You finish your allowed screen time and turn the device off even though you want to keep playing. (Responsible.)
Scenario 8: You see someone being unkind to another student online. You ignore it and scroll past. (Irresponsible: bystanders who do nothing allow unkind behaviour to continue. Better to tell an adult.)

Part 2: Class discussion (8 minutes)

Step 2 (8 minutes): Review each scenario together. Spend extra time on Scenario 8. Ask: "Why does doing nothing count as irresponsible? What could you do instead?" Guide students toward: telling a trusted adult, not joining in, and not sharing or spreading the unkind message.

Part 3: Rule writing (5 minutes)

Step 3 (5 minutes): Each student writes or dictates two personal digital citizenship rules. One must be about privacy. One must be about kindness or respect. Encourage specific rules rather than general ones: "I will ask before sharing someone's photo" is better than "I will be nice."

## Real-World Connection

Schools, workplaces, and websites all have rules about how people should behave online. These rules exist because online communication can spread quickly and affect many people. A message sent to one person can be forwarded to hundreds in seconds. Taking care with what you say and share is not just politeness, it is a practical skill for living and working safely in a connected world.

## Check for Understanding

1. You want to share a funny video of your friend from a class party. Before sharing it, what should you do?
2. A message arrives from an unknown user with a link saying "click here for a free game." What do you do?
3. Someone leaves a mean comment on your work online. List two things you could do in response.

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Focus on two rules only: "Ask before sharing" and "Tell an adult if something feels wrong." Practise recognising just these two principles in simple scenarios.

**For students who want a challenge:** Design a short guide for a younger student explaining three digital citizenship rules. Use pictures and simple sentences. The guide should be something a Kindergarten student could understand.

## Summary

- A digital citizen is someone who uses technology responsibly, kindly, and safely.
- Words and images shared online affect real people. Think before you send.
- Personal information includes your name, address, location, photos, and passwords. Keep these private.
- If something online makes you feel worried or upset, tell a trusted adult.
