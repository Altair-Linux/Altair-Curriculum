## Introduction

Every day you follow steps without even thinking about it. You get dressed, brush your teeth, and pack your bag — always in a certain order. When we write those steps down so that anyone (or any computer) can follow them, we call it an **algorithm**. Computers need algorithms because they cannot guess — they only do exactly what they are told, in the exact order they are told.

## What We Will Learn

- What an algorithm is and why order matters.
- How to sequence steps so a task works correctly.
- What an event is and how it triggers the next action.
- What goes wrong when steps are in the wrong order.

## Core Explanation

An **algorithm** is a set of clear, ordered steps that solves a problem or completes a task.

**Sequence** means the steps happen one after another. The order matters. If you put on your shoes before your socks, something goes wrong!

**Events** are things that happen and cause the next step to start. When an alarm rings (event), you wake up (next step). When a button is pressed (event), a computer opens a program (next step).

Here is a simple algorithm for making a glass of juice:

```
Step 1: Get a clean glass.
Step 2: Open the juice carton.
Step 3: Pour juice into the glass.
Step 4: Put the carton away.
Step 5: Drink the juice.
```

Each step must happen in order. If you do Step 5 before Step 1, you have no glass to drink from!

**Why computers need this:** A computer reads instructions exactly as written. It cannot skip ahead or use common sense. That is why programmers must think carefully about the right sequence.

## Activity

**Scrambled Morning Routine** (25 minutes)

*Materials needed: one set of sequencing cards per pair, markers.*

**Step 1 — Set up (2 min):** Cut out or draw cards with these actions on them, one action per card: Wake up, Turn off the alarm, Get out of bed, Go to the bathroom, Get dressed, Eat breakfast, Pack your bag, Leave for school.

**Step 2 — Scramble and solve (8 min):** Shuffle the cards face down. Each pair flips them over and arranges them in the order that makes sense. Students discuss: "Could this step happen before that one? Why or why not?"

**Step 3 — Share and compare (5 min):** Two pairs compare their sequences. Are they the same? Discuss any differences. (Some steps can swap — getting dressed before or after eating breakfast can both work. Discuss why.)

**Step 4 — Event arrows (5 min):** Using a marker, draw an arrow from each card to the next. Say aloud: "When I finish this card, the event that starts the next card is…" For example: "When I turn off my alarm, that is the event that starts getting out of bed."

**Step 5 — Silly sequence (5 min):** Teacher deliberately puts one card in the wrong place. Students explain what goes wrong and fix the sequence.

## Real-World Connection

Recipes in a cookbook are algorithms — the steps must happen in order, or the food comes out wrong. Traffic lights follow a sequence too: red stops cars so people can walk, then green lets cars go safely. Computers in traffic lights follow an algorithm just like the one you built with your cards.

## Check for Understanding

1. A student puts "eat breakfast" as the very first step in their morning algorithm. What problem might this cause?
2. Name one event from your day that triggers you to do something next.
3. If a computer reads its instructions in the wrong order, what do you think will happen?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use picture cards instead of word cards. Work through the morning routine together as a class before asking pairs to do it independently.

**For students who want a challenge:** Write an algorithm for a more complex task such as making a sandwich or tidying a desk. Include at least one step that can only happen after two earlier steps are both done, and explain why.

**Cross-curricular link:** In PE, discuss the sequence of steps for a physical skill such as jumping or passing a ball. Are sports skills algorithms?

## Summary

- An algorithm is a set of clear, ordered steps for solving a problem.
- Sequence means the steps must happen in the right order — changing the order can break the algorithm.
- Events are things that happen to trigger the next step, just like a button press starts a computer action.
