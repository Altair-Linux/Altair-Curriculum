## Introduction

Imagine you asked a robot to make you a glass of water, but you forgot to tell it to pick up the glass first. The robot would try to pour water and the water would go everywhere. That is what happens when a computer gets instructions that are unclear or in the wrong order. Today we are going to practise writing and following instructions carefully, because that is exactly what every computer program does.

## What We Will Learn

- That instructions must be clear so anyone can follow them.
- That the order of instructions matters.
- What happens when a step is missing or in the wrong order.
- How to write instructions that are simple enough for a computer to follow.

## Core Explanation

A computer program is a list of instructions. The computer reads each instruction one at a time, in order, from the top to the bottom. It does exactly what each instruction says. It does not guess. It does not skip steps. It does not use common sense.

This means instructions have to be very precise. "Be nice to the dog" is not an instruction a computer could follow because "be nice" means different things to different people. "Pat the dog gently on the head three times" is a precise instruction.

Instructions also have to be in the right order. If you are making a jam sandwich, you must get the bread before you spread the jam. If you try to spread jam before you have any bread, the step cannot work.

When we write instructions that a computer can follow, we call that writing an **algorithm**. An algorithm is just a fancy word for a clear list of steps in the right order that solves a problem or completes a task.

## Activity

**Instruction Scramble** (25 minutes)

This activity uses a simple task: washing hands. Write or print the following steps on separate strips of paper, one set per pair.

- Turn on the tap
- Get your hands wet
- Put soap on your hands
- Rub your hands together for 20 seconds
- Rinse the soap off your hands
- Turn off the tap
- Dry your hands with a towel

Step 1 (2 minutes): Give each pair a set of scrambled strips. Do not tell them the correct order. Ask them to arrange the strips in the order that makes sense.

Step 2 (8 minutes): Pairs arrange the strips and stick or lay them in order on their table. Walk around and observe. Do not correct yet.

Step 3 (5 minutes): Ask two or three pairs to read their order aloud. As a class, discuss any differences. Ask: "What would happen if you dried your hands before you turned on the tap?"

Step 4 (5 minutes): As a class, agree on the correct order. Ask students: "Could a robot follow these instructions exactly as written? Is there anything unclear?" For example, "Rub your hands together" is slightly vague. How long? This is where "for 20 seconds" makes it precise.

Step 5 (5 minutes): Introduce the prop robot or LEGO figure if using one. Say: "Pretend this robot only does exactly what I say." Give a deliberately wrong or silly instruction, such as "Dry your hands" first. Let students see why it fails. Then give the correct sequence and ask the class to act it out together.

## Real-World Connection

Every app on a phone or tablet is a program made of instructions. When you tap a button to open a game, the phone reads through its list of instructions and follows them in order. If the programmer wrote any step out of order, the game would crash or behave strangely. Getting the order right is one of the most important jobs a programmer has.

## Check for Understanding

1. You want to tell a robot how to pour a glass of juice. Write or draw three steps in the correct order.
2. A student puts "drink the milk" as the very first step in making a glass of milk. What has gone wrong?
3. Why is it important that instructions are precise, not just general?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use a two-step or three-step task instead of seven steps. For example: get a book, open the book, read the book. Have the student act out each step physically as they arrange the cards.

**For students who want a challenge:** Write a set of instructions for something they do at home, such as making toast or brushing teeth. They must include at least five steps. Then swap with a partner who must try to follow the instructions exactly and spot any missing or unclear steps.

## Summary

- A computer follows instructions one step at a time, in order.
- Instructions must be clear and precise. Vague instructions do not work.
- The order matters. Doing steps out of order leads to mistakes or failure.
- A clear list of steps that solves a problem is called an algorithm.
