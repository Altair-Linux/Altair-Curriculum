## Introduction

In Year 1, you collected data as a class and read information from a chart. This year you are going to design your own data collection, choose the question, decide the categories, record the data as a tally, convert it to a bar chart, and then answer questions from the chart yourself. This is the same process that scientists, doctors, and engineers use when they gather information about the world. The only difference is the scale. They might collect millions of data points. You will collect enough to practise the method.

## What We Will Learn

- How to write a clear data collection question with defined categories.
- How to record data using a tally and convert the tally to totals.
- How to draw a bar chart with a labelled axis and accurate bar heights.
- How to read a bar chart to compare values and draw a conclusion.

## Core Explanation

**Designing a data collection**

A good data question has one clear answer per person and categories that cover all possibilities without overlapping. "What is your favourite season?" is a good question. The categories are summer, autumn, winter, spring: four options, no overlaps, everyone has one answer. "Do you like school?" is too vague: what counts as liking it?

**Recording with a tally**

A tally uses vertical marks with a diagonal through every fifth mark. Groups of five make counting faster. After collecting all responses, you count the marks in each category and write the total.

**Drawing a bar chart**

A bar chart has two axes. The horizontal axis (along the bottom) lists the categories. The vertical axis (up the side) shows numbers from 0 to a value slightly above the highest tally count. Each bar is drawn to the height matching the count for that category. Bars are the same width and do not touch each other. The chart needs a title and both axes need labels.

**Reading the chart**

Once the chart is drawn, you can answer questions without counting the raw data again. The tallest bar is the most common. The shortest bar is the least common. The difference between two bars is found by subtracting.

## Activity

**Design, Collect, Chart, Conclude** (35 minutes)

Part 1: Class data collection (15 minutes)

Step 1 (5 minutes): Together, decide on a class data question. Suggested options: favourite school subject (literacy, maths, science, art), how you get to school (walk, car, bus, cycle), favourite meal of the day (breakfast, lunch, dinner). Write the question and the three or four categories on the board.

Step 2 (5 minutes): Each student gets a tally sheet. They walk around the room and ask five classmates the question, recording one tally mark per response in the correct category. (The teacher collects a class-wide total on the board as students share their tallies at the end.)

Step 3 (5 minutes): Each student counts their own tally marks per category and writes the totals. The teacher adds up the class totals on the board so students can check their individual tallies against the whole class picture.

Part 2: Drawing the bar chart (12 minutes)

Step 4 (3 minutes): Show the class how to set up the vertical axis. The highest total in the class data determines the top of the axis. If the highest count is 14, the axis goes from 0 to 15. Mark intervals of 2 or 5 depending on the scale.

Step 5 (9 minutes): Each student draws their bar chart using their own collected data (five responses) rather than the class total. They must: write a title, label both axes, and draw one bar per category reaching the correct height.

Part 3: Reading and concluding (8 minutes)

Step 6 (4 minutes): Each student answers three questions about their own chart in writing.
1. Which category had the most responses?
2. Which had the fewest?
3. How many more responses did the most popular category have than the least popular?

Step 7 (4 minutes): Each student writes one conclusion in a full sentence. It must use the word "because" and reference specific numbers from the chart. For example: "Most students I asked prefer lunch because it had 3 responses, which is more than breakfast (1) and dinner (1)."

## Real-World Connection

Hospitals track how many patients arrive each hour of the day. They draw charts to see which hours are busiest. They use those charts to decide how many staff to schedule. Without the chart, a manager would have to read hundreds of individual records to notice the pattern. The chart makes the pattern visible in seconds.

## Check for Understanding

1. A bar chart has four bars: red = 6, blue = 9, green = 3, yellow = 6. Which colour is most common? What is the difference between blue and green?
2. A student asks "Do you like fruit?" and gives only yes and no as options. Name one problem with this as a data collection question.
3. Why do you need to label the vertical axis on a bar chart? What would happen if it had no numbers?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Provide a pre-drawn bar chart template with the vertical axis already numbered and one category already filled in as an example. Students only need to add the remaining bars.

**For students who want a challenge:** Collect data on two questions simultaneously (for example, favourite fruit AND favourite vegetable). Draw two charts side by side and write one conclusion comparing the patterns across both charts.

## Summary

- A good data question has clear categories that do not overlap and cover all likely answers.
- A tally records responses efficiently and groups marks in fives to make counting easy.
- A bar chart shows data visually, making it easy to compare categories at a glance.
- Reading a chart means answering comparison questions and drawing conclusions that are supported by specific numbers.
