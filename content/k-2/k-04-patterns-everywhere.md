## Introduction

Look at a zebra. Black, white, black, white, black, white. Look at a traffic light. Red, then amber, then green, then red again. Look at the days of the week. Monday, Tuesday, Wednesday, then it starts over the following week. Patterns are everywhere, and computers use them constantly. When a programmer notices a pattern, they can write one short instruction instead of writing the same thing over and over. Today we are going to find patterns, describe them, and start thinking about why they matter.

## What We Will Learn

- What a pattern is.
- How to describe a repeating pattern.
- How to predict the next item in a pattern.
- Why patterns matter in computing.

## Core Explanation

A **pattern** is something that repeats in a predictable way. Once you know the repeating unit, you can predict what comes next.

The simplest patterns use two things. Red, blue, red, blue, red, blue. We call this an AB pattern because A (red) and B (blue) take turns. The repeating unit is "red, blue."

Some patterns use three things. Circle, circle, triangle, circle, circle, triangle. The repeating unit is "circle, circle, triangle." This is an AAB pattern.

Patterns do not have to be colours or shapes. They can be sounds: clap, clap, stomp, clap, clap, stomp. They can be movements: jump, turn, jump, turn. They can be numbers: 2, 4, 6, 8.

Computers are very good at patterns. When a computer has to do the same set of steps many times, it uses a pattern. Instead of writing the steps out ten times, the programmer writes them once and tells the computer to repeat the pattern. We will learn more about that in later lessons (it is called a loop). For now, the key skill is recognising the pattern and describing it clearly.

## Activity

**Pattern Building and Breaking** (25 minutes)

Materials: coloured blocks or counters in two or three colours per group.

Step 1 (5 minutes): Teacher demonstrates on the whiteboard. Draw or place: red, blue, red, blue, red. Ask: "What comes next? How do you know?" Draw the pattern unit in a box. "The unit is red, blue. It repeats."

Then do a harder pattern: yellow, yellow, green, yellow, yellow, green, yellow, yellow. Ask: "What comes next?" Draw the repeating unit: yellow, yellow, green.

Step 2 (10 minutes): In pairs, students build their own pattern using blocks. Rules: the pattern must repeat at least three full times. It must use at least two colours. When finished, they hide the last two pieces under their hand. Their partner must predict what is under the hand.

Step 3 (5 minutes): Each pair describes their pattern to another pair. They must use words: "Our pattern is red, blue, blue, red, blue, blue. The repeating unit is red, blue, blue."

Step 4 (5 minutes): Teacher creates a broken pattern on the board. For example: red, blue, red, red, blue, red, blue. Ask: "Something is wrong. Can you find where the pattern breaks?" Students point to the error.

**Clapping patterns (bonus, 5 minutes):** Teacher claps a rhythm pattern. Students join in, then continue it after the teacher stops. This shows that patterns exist in sound and time, not just in objects.

## Real-World Connection

Computer screens use patterns of tiny coloured dots called pixels. Every image on a screen is made of millions of pixels arranged in a pattern. The computer stores those patterns so it can show the same image again and again. When you send a photo to a friend, you are sending a pattern of colour information that the other screen can read.

## Check for Understanding

1. Here is a pattern: square, circle, circle, square, circle, circle. What comes next? What is the repeating unit?
2. Make up a three-step clapping pattern and teach it to the person next to you.
3. Why might a computer programmer be glad to find a pattern in the steps they need to write?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use physical objects the student can touch and move. Stick to AB patterns only. Use words like "the pattern goes A, B, A, B." Focus on finding one repeating unit before predicting.

**For students who want a challenge:** Create a pattern that changes in a non-repeating way, such as 1, 2, 4, 8, 16. Can you describe the rule? (Each number doubles.) This is a growing pattern rather than a repeating pattern. How is it different?

## Summary

- A pattern is something that repeats in a predictable way.
- The repeating unit is the part that keeps coming back.
- Describing a pattern clearly means you can predict what comes next.
- Computers use patterns to avoid doing the same work over and over.
