## Introduction

A honeybee builds a honeycomb using hexagons. Every cell is the same shape and fits perfectly with its neighbours. A pine cone arranges its scales in two overlapping spirals. A sunflower packs its seeds in the same spiral pattern. These are not accidents. These patterns appear because they are efficient: they use the least material, or pack the most in, or grow in a way that works. Computers use patterns for similar reasons. Instead of storing every pixel of an image separately, a computer finds the repeating unit and stores that once. Today we are going to look at patterns in nature and connect them to how computers store and create images.

## What We Will Learn

- That patterns appear throughout the natural world, often for practical reasons.
- How to describe a pattern by its repeating unit and rule.
- How computers use repeating rules to create images and save storage.
- How to design a tile pattern using a simple rule.

## Core Explanation

A natural pattern has a repeating unit and a rule for how it repeats. A zebra's stripes alternate between black and white bands. The rule is: black band, white band, repeat. A honeycomb uses hexagons packed edge to edge. The rule is: one hexagon surrounded by six more of the same size.

These patterns are efficient. Hexagons pack together with no wasted space, which is why bees use them. Stripes are created by groups of cells that switch off and on during growth, like a biological loop.

Computers use patterns in images. A digital image is made of tiny squares called pixels. Each pixel has a colour. If a section of an image is all one colour, the computer does not need to store each pixel individually. It can store one instruction: "colour this section blue." This is a form of pattern recognition that makes files smaller.

When you create a repeating tile pattern, you are doing what a computer does when it generates a background texture. You design one tile (the repeating unit), then apply the rule: place this tile left to right, top to bottom, across the whole surface.

## Activity

**Pattern Hunt and Tile Design** (30 minutes)

Part 1: Pattern hunt (10 minutes)

Step 1 (5 minutes): Display five images of natural patterns. For each one, ask students to answer two questions. First, what is the repeating unit? Second, what is the rule for how the units connect or alternate?

Zebra: repeating unit is one black band and one white band. Rule: alternate left to right.
Honeycomb: repeating unit is one hexagon. Rule: packed tightly with six neighbours each.
Sunflower seeds: repeating unit is one seed. Rule: placed in two overlapping spiral directions.
Snake scales: repeating unit is one oval scale. Rule: rows offset so each row fits between the one above.
Chessboard: repeating unit is two squares, one black and one white. Rule: alternate in both directions.

Step 2 (5 minutes): Ask: "Why do you think these patterns appear in nature rather than random arrangements?" Accept answers and guide towards: efficiency, strength, growth rules. Compare to a computer: "A computer uses patterns to save space and work faster."

Part 2: Tile design (20 minutes)

Step 3 (5 minutes): Show how a tile works on squared paper. On a 3x3 grid, colour some squares red and some blue. Then show that grid repeated four times across and three times down. The result is a repeating pattern.

Step 4 (10 minutes): Each student designs their own tile on a 4x4 section of squared paper using two or three colours. They colour the tile, then extend the pattern to fill a larger area by repeating the tile.

Step 5 (5 minutes): Students write or dictate the rule for their tile. "My tile is four squares wide and four squares tall. The top row is red, blue, red, blue. The second row is blue, red, blue, red. This is called a checkerboard rule."

Students swap tiles with a partner and try to extend the partner's tile pattern using only the written rule, without looking at the extended version.

## Real-World Connection

Fabric designers create one small tile pattern, then a machine repeats it thousands of times across metres of cloth. Video game designers create one grass tile or one brick tile, then the game engine tiles it to fill an entire landscape. The same tile approach is used for bathroom floors, wallpaper, and wrapping paper. One design, infinite repeats.

## Check for Understanding

1. Look at a chessboard. What is the repeating unit? What is the rule for how the unit extends across the board?
2. A designer creates a 2x2 tile that is half red and half blue. If this tile is repeated 10 times across and 10 times down, how many tiles appear in total?
3. How does a computer save storage space by using patterns instead of storing every pixel individually?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use a 2x2 tile with only two colours. Work side by side to colour four repeats of the tile on paper. Focus on identifying the tile boundary and repeating it accurately.

**For students who want a challenge:** Create a tile that looks different when rotated. Repeat it in alternating orientations (every other tile rotated 90 degrees) and see how the overall pattern changes. Describe the new rule.

## Summary

- Patterns in nature have a repeating unit and a rule for how that unit extends.
- These patterns often appear because they are efficient: they pack well, grow predictably, or use minimal material.
- Computers use repeating patterns to create images and to store information more efficiently.
- Designing a tile and extending it by rule is the same principle computers use for textures and backgrounds.
