## Introduction

When something is difficult, one of the best things you can do is break it into smaller pieces. A big puzzle looks impossible until you separate the pieces into corners, edges, and middles. A long journey is easier when you know the first turning. Problem solving is a skill that gets better with practice. Computers solve problems using the same ideas: break the problem down, try something, check if it worked, and fix it if it did not. This lesson is your chance to practise all of those things.

## What We Will Learn

- That big problems are easier when you break them into smaller parts.
- How to try something, check if it worked, and try again with a change.
- That making a mistake is part of solving a problem, not a sign of failure.
- How to describe the steps you took to reach a solution.

## Core Explanation

**Breaking problems into parts**

When a problem feels too big, start by asking: what is the smallest piece I can solve first? For a tangram, start with one shape in one corner. For a maze, look one step ahead rather than trying to plan the whole route at once.

Programmers call this **decomposition**. It means breaking a big problem into smaller ones. Instead of thinking "I need to build a whole app," a programmer thinks "I need to make the button work first." Then the next part. Then the next.

**Try, check, fix**

When you try something and it does not work, you have learned something useful. Now you know one thing that does not work, which brings you closer to finding what does. This is called **debugging** in computing. A programmer writes some instructions, runs them, sees what goes wrong, and changes the instructions. They repeat this until the program works.

Good problem solvers do not give up when their first idea fails. They ask: what went wrong? Can I change just one thing and try again?

**Describing your steps**

After solving a problem, it helps to think about how you did it. What did you try first? What changed when it did not work? Being able to describe your steps means you can solve a similar problem next time, and you can help someone else too.

## Activity

**Puzzle Stations** (30 minutes)

Set up two or three puzzle stations around the room. Each station has a different challenge.

Station 1 (Tangram or shape puzzle): Students must fit a set of shapes into an outlined silhouette. They try, adjust, try again.

Station 2 (Copy a pattern): Students look at a peg board pattern or LEGO model photo and try to replicate it. If they get stuck, they cover the image and rebuild from memory, then compare.

Station 3 (Maze, printed): Students find their way through a maze. When they hit a dead end, they go back to the last decision point and try a different path.

Step 1 (2 minutes): Introduce all three stations. Explain: "You will visit at least two stations. At each one, try to solve the puzzle. If you get stuck, ask yourself: what is the smallest part I can solve right now?"

Step 2 (20 minutes): Students rotate through stations in small groups. Teacher circulates and asks questions: "What did you try? What happened? What will you try next?"

Step 3 (8 minutes): Bring the class together. Ask three students to share: "What was your problem? What did you try first? What changed when it did not work?" Write their answers on the board in three columns: Problem, First Try, What I Changed.

Highlight that every student made at least one change. Say: "Every time you changed something and tried again, you were doing exactly what a programmer does. That process is called debugging."

## Real-World Connection

When an engineer builds a bridge, they test a small model first. If the model bends or breaks, they change the design before building the real thing. Scientists do the same with experiments. Trying, checking, and fixing is how almost everything in the world gets built and improved.

## Check for Understanding

1. You are stuck on a puzzle and your first idea did not work. What are two things you could do next?
2. What does it mean to break a problem into smaller parts? Give an example.
3. Why is making a mistake useful when you are solving a problem?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Sit with the student at one station. Narrate your own thinking aloud: "I am going to try this piece here. Does it fit? No. What if I turn it? Let me try." This models the try-and-fix process explicitly.

**For students who want a challenge:** After solving a puzzle, write or draw the instructions someone else would need to solve the same puzzle. Can your instructions help a friend who has never seen the puzzle before?

## Summary

- Break big problems into smaller parts. Solve one part at a time.
- Try something, check if it worked, and change it if it did not. This is called debugging.
- Making a mistake is not failure. It is information that helps you find the right answer.
- Describing your steps helps you and others solve similar problems in the future.
