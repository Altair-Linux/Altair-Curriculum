## Introduction

Computers and tablets are tools, just like scissors or paint. When you use scissors you hold them carefully and walk, not run. When you use a device, there are also rules that keep you safe and keep the device working well. Some of those rules are about the physical device, and some are about the information you share when you use it. Today we are going to learn the most important rules so that using devices is always a good experience.

## What We Will Learn

- How to look after a device so it does not get damaged.
- That some information is private and belongs to you.
- Who a trusted adult is and when to ask one for help.
- What to do if something online makes you feel worried or uncomfortable.

## Core Explanation

**Looking after devices**

Devices are expensive and can break easily. Here are the rules that matter most.

Use dry hands before touching a tablet or keyboard. Water and electronics do not mix. Hold a device with two hands so it does not drop. Keep food and drinks away. When you are finished, put the device back in its place carefully.

**Keeping information private**

When you use a device, sometimes a screen might ask for your name, age, or address. This information is private. Private means it belongs to you and your family, and you should not share it with strangers, even if they seem friendly. Your full name, home address, school name, and phone number are all private.

The internet connects you to other people all over the world. Most people are kind, but you cannot tell by looking at a screen whether someone is being honest. This is why we share private information only with people we already know and trust in real life.

**Asking a trusted adult**

A trusted adult is a person in your life who you know well and who keeps you safe. This could be a parent, carer, teacher, or another family member. Whenever you want to do something new on a device, visit a new website, or download something, ask a trusted adult first. If something on a screen makes you feel strange, worried, or unhappy, stop and tell a trusted adult straight away.

## Activity

**Safe or Not Safe?** (20 minutes)

This activity uses scenarios described aloud (or shown as simple pictures if available).

Step 1 (3 minutes): Ask students to show a thumbs up for "safe" and a thumbs down for "not safe." Practice the signals with a simple example: "Washing your hands before eating. Safe or not safe?" (Thumbs up.)

Step 2 (12 minutes): Read each scenario slowly. After each one, ask students to show their signal and then ask one or two students to explain their choice.

Scenario 1: Mia is using a tablet and her hands are clean and dry. (Safe.)
Scenario 2: Sam is eating a biscuit with one hand and holding the tablet with the other. (Not safe. Crumbs and grease can damage the screen.)
Scenario 3: A game asks for your full name and your home address. You type it in. (Not safe. That information is private.)
Scenario 4: You see something on the screen that makes you feel worried. You close the tablet and tell your teacher. (Safe.)
Scenario 5: A message pops up saying "you have won a prize, click here." You click it without asking an adult. (Not safe.)
Scenario 6: You are finished using the tablet. You carry it with two hands and put it back in the charging tray. (Safe.)

Step 3 (5 minutes): As a class, make a short list of three rules to put on the wall. Keep them short and positive. For example: "Dry hands only. Keep it safe." "Private information stays private." "When in doubt, ask an adult."

## Real-World Connection

Just like you would not give a stranger your house key, you do not give strangers your personal information online. The same instinct that tells you to be careful in an unfamiliar place applies online too. Your trusted adults are there to help you make safe choices.

## Check for Understanding

1. Your friend asks you to type your home address into a game. What do you do?
2. Name two ways you can look after a device so it does not break.
3. If something on a screen makes you feel upset, what is the first thing you should do?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Focus only on the device care rules. Use a real device to physically practise carrying with two hands, putting it down gently, and checking that hands are dry. Repeat the phrase "ask an adult first" for any online decision.

**For students who want a challenge:** Draw a poster with two columns: "Safe choices" and "Choices that need an adult." Add as many examples as you can think of. Share the poster with a younger student or family member.

## Summary

- Look after devices by using dry hands, holding them carefully, and putting them away properly.
- Some information is private: your full name, address, school name, and phone number.
- Always ask a trusted adult before doing something new on a device.
- If something makes you feel worried or uncomfortable online, stop and tell a trusted adult.
