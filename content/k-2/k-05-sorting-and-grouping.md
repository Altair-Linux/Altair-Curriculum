## Introduction

Imagine your teacher asked you to find the blue crayon in a box where all the crayons were mixed up in no order. You would have to look at every single one. Now imagine the crayons were sorted by colour first. You would go straight to the blue section. Sorting makes finding things much faster. Computers sort enormous amounts of information every second so they can find what they need quickly. Today we are going to practise sorting and find out why the rule matters.

## What We Will Learn

- What it means to sort and group objects.
- That sorting always uses a rule, such as colour, size, or shape.
- That the same objects can be sorted in different ways depending on the rule.
- Why computers sort information before searching through it.

## Core Explanation

**Sorting** means putting things in order or into groups based on a rule. The rule tells you what to look at. Common rules include colour (put all the red ones together), size (put all the big ones together), and shape (put all the circles together).

**Grouping** means putting things that share something into the same pile or set.

The same set of objects can be sorted in many different ways. A collection of buttons could be sorted by colour, by number of holes, by size, or by whether they are round or square. The buttons do not change. The rule changes.

When you sort, you look at one attribute at a time. An attribute is a feature or property. Colour is an attribute. Size is an attribute. Shape is an attribute.

Computers sort data all the time. When you search for a song, the computer sorts through millions of song titles and finds the one that matches. When you open your contacts, the phone has sorted them alphabetically so you can find your friend quickly. Sorting is one of the most common tasks a computer does.

## Activity

**The Great Sort** (25 minutes)

Materials: one set of 12 to 16 mixed objects per group (buttons, shapes, blocks), sorting areas labelled with the rule.

Step 1 (3 minutes): Pour out the collection on the table. Ask students: "What do you notice? Can you find any that are alike?" Let them look freely for one minute.

Step 2 (5 minutes): Give the first rule: "Sort by colour." Groups sort their objects into colour groups. When done, ask: "How many groups did you make? Does any object belong to more than one group?" (It should not under a single-attribute rule.)

Step 3 (5 minutes): Now give a different rule: "Sort by size. Big, medium, small." Students re-sort the same objects. Ask: "Did any object move to a different group this time?"

Step 4 (5 minutes): Choose one student to make up their own rule without telling the class. They sort the objects silently. The rest of the group watches and tries to guess the rule. Say: "What is their sorting rule?"

Step 5 (7 minutes): Bring the class together. Ask: "If I had a box with 100 objects and I needed to find the red ones quickly, should I sort first or just search through all 100?" Discuss. Then ask: "What if I changed my mind and needed the big ones instead? Would my previous sort help or not?" This leads students towards understanding that sorting depends on knowing what you need first.

## Real-World Connection

When you save a photo on a tablet, the device sorts it by date so you can scroll back in time to find an old photo. Music apps sort songs by artist, album, or genre. Libraries sort books by subject and author name. Computers and people both sort things to save time later.

## Check for Understanding

1. Here are five shapes: red circle, blue circle, red square, blue triangle, red triangle. Sort them by colour. How many groups do you have?
2. Now sort the same five shapes by shape. How many groups do you have now?
3. Why is it useful to sort things before you search for something?

## Extension and Differentiation

**For students who need more support:** Use a simple set with only two attributes: red or blue, big or small. Sort only by colour first, then only by size. Ask: "Does this one go with the reds or the blues?"

**For students who want a challenge:** Try sorting the same set by two rules at the same time. For example: big AND red in one group, big AND blue in another, small AND red in another, small AND blue in another. This is called sorting by two attributes. How many groups do you end up with?

## Summary

- Sorting means putting things into groups using a rule.
- The rule decides what attribute to look at: colour, size, shape, and so on.
- The same objects can be sorted in different ways by changing the rule.
- Computers sort information so they can find things quickly.
